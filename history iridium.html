History Of Iridium

In 1800, the British chemist Smithson Tennant began an association (in London, England) with another chemist named W.H. Wollaston. The purpose of the association was to improve the technology for fabricating platinum, and it was observed that when platinum was dissolved in aqua regia, a small black residue always remained. Tennant began to study this black residue in 1803, and by the following year, he discovered the element iridim, as well as another element, called osmium. Iridium was the name given by Tennant for this element, which comes from the Greek word iris (rainbow). It was named for the brilliant colors of many of its compunds (some of which include iridium chloride (IrCl), iridium di-iodide (IrI(2), and di-iridium trioxide (Ir(2)O(3)).
